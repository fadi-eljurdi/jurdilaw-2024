Foreign Ownership of Real Estate in Lebanon in 2025


Lebanon?s real estate market has attracted international investors for a long time. They seek opportunities in one of the Middle East?s most culturally rich countries.

However, strict legal regulations govern foreigners buying property in Lebanon. If you are not Lebanese and want to invest in real estate, it is important to understand the laws. This will help you follow the rules and protect your assets.


Legal Foundation: Decree No. 11614 and Law No. 296


The main legal reference for foreign property ownership in Lebanon is Decree No. 11614. The authorities issued this decree on January 4, 1969. Law No. 296 changed it on April 3, 2001.

These laws explain how foreigners can buy property in Lebanon. They base themselves on Lebanon's constitutional principles. These principles include protecting national sovereignty and banning resettlement.


Real Estate Laws for Foreigners in Lebanon


1. License Requirement


1. License Requirement

Before purchasing property in Lebanon, foreigners must obtain a license from the Ministry of Finance. This license is essential as it grants permission to buy real estate.

The application process involves submitting various documents, including proof of identity and the intended use of the property. A good idea is to work with a local lawyer. You can rely on them to help you through this process and ensure that you complete all paperwork correctly.


2. Property Type Restrictions

Foreigners are allowed to buy property in Lebanon, but there are restrictions on the type and size of the property. For instance, non-Lebanese individuals can only purchase a maximum of 3,000 square meters of land. Additionally, certain areas may have specific regulations that limit foreign ownership, particularly in regions deemed sensitive for national security or cultural heritage.


3. Investment Purpose

When applying for a license, you must specify the purpose of your investment. Whether you plan to use the property for residential, commercial, or tourism-related activities, this information is crucial. The authorities may prioritize applications that align with national interests, such as those that promote tourism or economic development.


4. Taxes and Fees

Investing in real estate in Lebanon comes with various taxes and fees. These may include property transfer taxes, registration fees, and annual property taxes. Budgeting for these costs is important, as they can significantly impact your overall investment. Consulting with a financial advisor familiar with Lebanese real estate can help you understand these obligations.


5. Legal Assistance

Navigating the legal landscape of real estate in Lebanon can be complex, especially for foreigners. I highly recommend hiring a local attorney who specializes in real estate law. They can help you understand your rights, negotiate contracts, and ensure compliance with all legal requirements. This support can be invaluable in avoiding potential pitfalls and ensuring a smooth transaction.


6. Market Research

Before making any investment, conducting thorough market research is essential. Knowing the local real estate market is important.

This includes property values, demand trends, and neighborhood dynamics. Understanding these factors will help you make better decisions. Engaging with local real estate agents can provide insights into the best areas for investment and potential growth opportunities.

2. Restrictions on Certain Buyers


Property acquisition is prohibited for:

  • Stateless individuals are those not belonging to a recognized country.
  • Entities or individuals whose acquisitions violate the constitutional ban on permanent settlement in Lebanon.


3. Ownership Limits by Area


Foreign ownership is limited by law to:

  • 3% of Lebanon?s total land area.
  • 10% of land in Beirut Governorate.


These caps include companies deemed ?non-Lebanese? under Article 2 of Decree No. 11614. Exceptions apply for Lebanese-majority-owned companies with share transfer restrictions.

When the Council of Ministers reaches the limits, it may suspend further foreign acquisitions. The Ministry of Finance publishes statistics biannually in the Official Gazette.


4. Development Obligation


Foreign-owned property must be developed or used for its intended purpose within five years of acquisition. If not, the Ministry of Finance may reclaim or force the sale of the property.


5. Licensing Exemptions


No license is required in cases such as:

  • Land under 3,000 sqm for construction.
  • Property acquired via inheritance or donations among heirs.
  • Specific rights like usufruct, mortgages, and leases (under regulated terms).


Constitutional Council?s Position


In Decision No. 2/2001, Lebanon?s Constitutional Council confirmed that foreign ownership restrictions

  • Are based on national sovereignty and public interest.
  • Do not contradict international human rights obligations.
  • Align with the constitutional ban on permanent resettlement.


This decision remains a guiding precedent, reinforcing the legitimacy of property laws restricting foreign ownership.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)


Can a foreigner buy property in Lebanon?


Yes, they must get a license from the Council of Ministers. They also need to follow ownership limits and development rules.


What are the area limits for foreign property ownership?


Foreigners can own

  • Up to 3% of Lebanon?s total land area.
  • Up to 10% in Beirut Governorate.


Are there exemptions to the licensing rule?


Yes. Exemptions include:

  • Plots under 3,000 sqm for construction.
  • Inherited or gifted properties among family.
  • Specific use rights (e.g., usufruct, leases, mortgages).


What happens if I don?t develop the property?


Someone must use or develop the property within five years. If not, the Ministry of Finance may seize or sell it.


Can companies owned by Lebanese and foreigners buy property?


Yes, but if non-Lebanese own most shares, people see the company as foreign. It must follow the same rules.


Do Lebanese laws conflict with international human rights law?


No. The Constitutional Council ruled that Lebanon?s property restrictions are based on legitimate public interest and national sovereignty.
