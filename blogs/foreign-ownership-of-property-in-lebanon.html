FOREIGNER OWNERSHIP OF PROPERTY IN LEBANON


Lebanon&#x2019;s real estate market presents lucrative opportunities for international investors, but navigating the country&#x2019;s property laws can be complex. For non-Lebanese individuals and companies considering property acquisitions in Lebanon, understanding the legal framework is essential to ensure compliance and maximize investment potential.



??A. Understanding the Legal Framework - Decree No. 11614 (January 4, 1969)


Lebanon?s property laws regulate the acquisition of real property rights by non-Lebanese through a series of decrees and amendments. The foundational legal document is Decree No. 11614 (January 4, 1969), which has been amended by subsequent laws, most notably Law No. 296 (April 3, 2001). These laws set strict conditions and limits for property ownership by foreigners, reflecting Lebanon?s commitment to sovereignty and constitutional provisions against settlement. So, what are Key Provisions for Non-Lebanese Property Ownership &#x203D;


1. Licensing Requirement


Non-Lebanese individuals or entities must obtain a license from the Lebanese Council of Ministers before acquiring any real property rights. This includes ownership of land or property designated for construction. Licenses are granted upon the proposal of the Minister of Finance and are subject to specific conditions outlined by law.  


2.Prohibited Transactions


The law prohibits property acquisitions by:  


  • Individuals without nationality from a recognized state.  
  • Acquisitions that contravene Lebanon?s constitutional ban on settlement.  


3.Ownership Limits 


Non-Lebanese ownership is by Law capped at:  


  • 3% of the total land area in Lebanon.
  • 10% of the total land area in Beirut Governorate.  


The ownership percentages mentioned above include Lebanese companies considered equivalent to non-Lebanese under Article 2 of the law enacted by Decree No. 11614 dated 4/1/1969, with the following exceptions:


First: Partnerships and Limited Liability Companies where the majority of shares (more than 50%) are owned by natural Lebanese partners or purely Lebanese companies, provided their bylaws prohibit the transfer of these shares to non-Lebanese persons. In such cases, only 50% of the areas owned by these companies are calculated within the percentages mentioned in this article.


Second: Joint Stock Companies or Partnerships Limited by Stocks where the majority of stocks (more than 50%) are owned by natural Lebanese persons or purely Lebanese companies, provided their statutes prohibit the transfer of these shares to non-Lebanese persons. In such cases, only 50% of the areas owned by these companies are calculated within the percentages mentioned in this article.


The attainment of the ownership percentages will be announced by a decree adopted by the Council of Ministers upon the proposal of the Minister of Finance. The acquisition of real property rights by non-Lebanese persons, or those deemed equivalent to non-Lebanese under Article 2 of the law enacted by Decree No. 11614 dated 4/1/1969, will be suspended unless such acquisitions occur between non-Lebanese persons or entities deemed equivalent.


Once the legal limits are reached, the Directorate of Real Estate Affairs at the Ministry of Finance will take the necessary measures to suspend the registration of real property rights for non-Lebanese persons. These statistics will be published in the Official Gazette every six months.


4.Development Obligation


Property acquired by non-Lebanese must be developed or allocated for its intended purpose within five years of registration. Failure to comply may result in forfeiture or forced sale by the Ministry of Finance.  


5.Exemptions From Licensing

  

Certain cases are exempt from licensing requirements, including:  


  • Properties under 3,000 square meters designated for construction.
  • Inherited properties or those acquired through wills and donations among heirs.
  • Specific rights such as usufruct [The right to use and enjoy something belonging to another, which is automatically extinguished upon the death of the usufructuary. (Article 32 of Decision 333 issued on November 12, 1926)] contracts, mortgages, and leasing under regulated conditions.  


B. Lebanese Constitutional Council Position on Article One Amendment


The Constitutional Council Decision No. 2/2001, while issued over two decades ago, remains a cornerstone in understanding Lebanon?s property laws. The decision upheld the legal restrictions on property ownership by non-Lebanese, emphasizing Lebanon?s sovereign right to regulate land ownership in alignment with its constitutional principles, including the prohibition of settlement.  


The Council affirmed that such restrictions do not violate Lebanon?s commitments to international human rights treaties, as they are based on legitimate public interest and national sovereignty. The decision highlights the critical importance of adhering to licensing and ownership limits, especially for foreign investors.
