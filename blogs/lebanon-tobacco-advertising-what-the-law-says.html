LEBANON TOBACCO ADVERTISING - WHAT THE LAW SAYS?

IS TOBACCO ADVERTISING ACTUALLY BANNED IN LEBANON?


Not long after the LATimes published an article titled &#x22;Lebanon May Follow Anti-Smoking Trend&#x22;, and precisely on August 29,2011, Law No. 174 (&#x22;Law&#x22;) relating to the Reduction of Smoking and Regulating the Manufacture, Packaging, and Advertising of Tobacco Products, was issued in Lebanon. Publiclically referred to at the time as  Lebanon Smoking Ban 2025, the Law was expected to be received with a lot of dissatisfied voices (people and traders) appearing to be a step in the right direction. 


Now with the beginning of 2025 and despite the challenges in the enforcement and the rise of heated tobacco and other emerging products (addressed partially by Decision 877/1 of 2015), Law No. 174 remains the primary legal framework governing tobacco promotion and advertising in Lebanon.


WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW ABOUT THE RULES FOR PROMOTING & ADVERTISING TOBACCO IN LEBANON?


Article 9 of the Law on Advertising and Promotions of Tobacco Products stipulates that it is prohibited to advertise or place advertisements for a tobacco product, a tobacco product brand, or any related distinctive mark or trademark, except under the conditions and deadlines specified in the Law and its implementing decrees. It is also prohibited to manufacture, import, promote, sell, or display any goods of any kind that represent or suggest, in any form, a tobacco product, including but not limited to foods, sweets, candies, and toys, particularly for minors.   


Manufacturers, importers, wholesalers, retailers, or distributors are prohibited from offering free gifts, in any form, to customers purchasing tobacco products or their derivatives. This includes gifts bearing logos, distinctive marks, trademarks, rights to participate in raffles, or any other competitions (&#x22;Article 10&#x22;).  


IS TOBACCO PRODUCT PLACEMENT IN MEDIA, ALLOWED UNDER THE LEBANESE LAW?


Article 11 prohibits all forms of tobacco product placement in the media. Broadcasting or publishing any material, whether paid or unpaid, that promotes tobacco is strictly forbidden across visual, audio, print, and electronic platforms. Notably, there are no exceptions for portrayals deemed &#x22;organic to the story.&#x22;


According to Tobacco Control Laws Platform:

&#x22;Although Law No. 174, effective March 2012, prohibits the broadcast of tobacco advertising and promotion, the law does not explicitly ban international or cross-border tobacco advertising on TV and radio. Therefore, it is uncertain whether the law bans international TV and radio advertising.

To align with FCTC Article 13 and the FCTC Article 13 Guidelines, the law should clearly state that all international and cross-border tobacco advertising and promotion, including via international TV and radio, is prohibited.&#x22;

This ambiguity extends to publications, leaving uncertainty about whether international newspapers and magazines featuring tobacco advertisements fall under the ban.

In the currently very complex media landscape, international advertising often bypasses domestic jurisdictions, and without explicit bans, law enforcement bodies may struggle to hold foreign entities accountable. However, does the above call for a law amendment or it is primarily a question of enforceability?


ARE THERE RESTRICTIONS ON OFFLINE TOBACCO ADVERTISING?


Lebanese law imposes strict regulations on offline tobacco advertising:

  • Billboards, building roofs, store facades, and other public displays of tobacco advertisements are prohibited.
  • Sponsorship of events, including cultural, sports, or commercial activities, by tobacco companies is strictly forbidden.

However, retailers are allowed to display signage within their establishments indicating tobacco products are sold there. Similarly, wholesalers may place signs on their storefronts identifying them as authorized distributors under the Lebanese Tobacco and Tombac Monopoly Administration (commonly known as &#x22;Regie&#x22;).


WHO ENFORCES & PROSECUTE TOBACCO LAWS VIOLATIONS IN LEBANON?


The law grants enforcement authority to:

  • Police officers,
  • Inspectors from the Ministry of Public Health,
  • The Consumer Protection Directorate at the Ministry of Economy and Trade,
  • The Tourism Police.

Any tobacco products found in violation of the law are confiscated and handed over to Regie along with the violation report.

Penalties for violators include fines and imprisonment ranging from one month to one year.


THE FUTURE OF TOBACCO BAN IN LEBANON


Despite enforceability obstacles and the need for further executive orders to fill in any gaps in the regulations, the Law remains to be a significant milestone that Lebanon took in 2011. With the rising popularity of tobacco amongst the younger generation, and youth tobacco use in Lebanon becoming a snowball of economic burden, Lebanon needs not only to find a quick solution to tobacco law enforcement challenges to tackle a phenomenon that is already costing the Lebanese economy billions of dollars desperately needed for the country?s economic development but also to issue further regulations, including but not limited to the imposing of a high excise tax. The World Health Organization recommends a minimum 75% tax share of the retail price of tobacco, which Lebanon remains way below. Tobacco excise taxes in Lebanon are well below these recommendations. that tackles in detail the use, mention and indirect advertising of tobacco products in media, a step that could have a true impact on the economic cost of tobacco consumption in Lebanon. 


With Lebanon now bound for economic recovery expected to take place following the electing of the new Lebanese President Joseph Aoun and the nomination of the new prime minister Nawaf Salam, legislations for long locked in the drawers of the Lebanese parliament are expected to move forward to get things up to speed with the huge and rapid changes in the Middle East region. Tobacco business being historically tied to politics and political decisions and with the potential emergence of better governance, law enforcement is expected to take precedence over other topics on the new government agenda. For that, businesses engaged in the tobacco business as well as others should stay tuned to upcoming changes in the legal landscape some of which impact the tobacco industry. With that in  mind businesses should regularly review new decisions or updates related to legislations and regulations impacting the tobacco industry to ensure compliance and your business might need to adapt to future regulations on emerging products like e-cigarettes and heated tobacco.
